The belief

"Antibody galactosylation
collapses at large scale
because CO2 builds up."

Is that actually true?
The instrument

GlycoTwin

A mechanistic digital twin built from public literature — no data fitting. A CHO bioreactor model feeds an ER→Golgi enzyme train, so a reactor knob propagates all the way to the antibody's sugar.

Two real levers: Golgi enzymes  &  nucleotide-sugar donors.

The experiment · bench → 2000 L

CO2 rises 60→74 mmHg — galactosylation barely moves (+1 pp)

The Golgi pH sits on the galactosyltransferase pH optimum (~6.4) — the flat top of its activity curve.
Control experiment · vary O2

Dissolved O2 moves galactosylation 0.0 pp — CO2, not O2

So what IS the lever?

The clone — B4GALT1 recovers +35 pp. Not the gas strategy.

Galactose feed & Mn2+ ~10 pp each · restoring CO2 stripping ~0 pp.
Why a mechanistic model

It gives the reason —
and the reason is testable at the bench.

The engineering implication is concrete: at scale-up, spend control effort on clone and media, not on chasing CO2.

Reproducible · open · agent-augmented

One script regenerates every result · 36 automated tests ·
a governed agent loop proposes recipes, each scored by the mechanism — never the model's opinion.

The finding

The mental model was wrong.
Galactosylation is robust to scale —
engineer the clone, not the gas.
